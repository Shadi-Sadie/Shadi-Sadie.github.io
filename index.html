About

I’m a health economist and data scientist focused on turning complex administrative and survey data into evidence that decision‑makers can act on. My current research examines how the Affordable Care Act’s Medicaid expansion changed coverage, access, and use of care for U.S.-born and foreign‑born adults. Methodologically, I work with modern difference‑in‑differences and event‑study designs that account for staggered policy timing, and I pair those designs with survey‑weighted estimation and design‑based standard errors. I enjoy the unglamorous parts of analysis—clean measurement, careful checks for pre‑trends, robustness to alternative cohorts and specifications, and transparent uncertainty communication. Beyond research, I’m motivated by work that lowers administrative burden and makes programs easier to use, particularly for immigrants and other groups who face documentation and language barriers. I have experience collaborating with clinicians and policy teams, teaching microeconomics and labor economics, and building lightweight tools in R and Python to share results. I value open, reproducible workflows, clear writing, and pragmatic recommendations that acknowledge trade‑offs. I’m based in Ann Arbor, MI, work primarily remotely, and am open to short travel for collaboration and presentations.

Methods of interest: identification in staggered adoption settings; survey design & variance estimation; budget impact modeling; decision‑useful evidence for payers and providers.

Research

Medicaid Expansion and Coverage by Nativity: Evidence from Modern DiD
Working paper (2025) — PDF · Slides

We evaluate how the Affordable Care Act’s Medicaid expansion affected insurance coverage for U.S.-born and foreign-born adults using the American Community Survey (2011–2019). Leveraging interaction‑weighted difference‑in‑differences estimators (Sun & Abraham), we decompose effects by cohort and event time, examine pre‑trend balance, and report cohort‑specific ATTs to address staggered adoption. We show where two‑way fixed effects would bias estimates and provide robustness checks (placebo cohorts, alternative timing, weighting). Results highlight larger gains among long‑residing foreign‑born adults and heterogeneous effects by state policy environment, with implications for outreach and administrative burden reduction.

Affordable Care Act and Cancer Screening among Foreign‑born vs Native
Working paper

This study investigates whether ACA‑era coverage expansions and cost‑sharing protections shifted preventive cancer screening among foreign‑born versus U.S.‑born adults. Using modern DiD with state‑by‑year staggered adoption, we analyze screening outcomes (mammography, Pap tests, and colorectal screening) with survey weights and design‑based standard errors. We explore heterogeneity by length of U.S. residence, English proficiency, and Medicaid expansion timing. Placebo timing and alternative functional forms are used to probe identification. The findings clarify where gains accrued, where gaps persisted, and which policy levers—insurance continuity, primary care access, and navigation supports—appear most salient.

The Impact of Medicaid Expansion on Health Outcomes: U.S.‑Born and Foreign‑Born Communities
Working paper

We examine downstream outcomes associated with Medicaid expansion—access to a usual source of care, emergency department visits, hospitalizations, and self‑reported health—stratified by nativity. Using MEPS and replicate weights, we estimate survey‑weighted GLMs and difference‑in‑differences/event‑study models with design‑based standard errors. We assess parallel trends in the pre‑period, probe sensitivity to cohort trimming and alternative exposure definitions, and present subgroup results by chronic condition status. The analysis distinguishes utilization changes from compositional shifts and discusses mechanisms (affordability, provider networks) alongside limitations, including limited power for rare outcomes and potential migration confounding.

Unequal Burdens: Analyzing Baltimore City’s Sales Tax Disparities (with P. Bennett)
Working paper

This paper maps neighborhood‑level differences in effective sales tax burden in Baltimore City, combining point‑of‑sale location data with demographic and income characteristics. We construct tract‑level exposure measures that reflect where residents actually shop, not just where they live, and examine associations with distance to retail clusters, transit access, and income. Results suggest more regressive effective burdens in transit‑sparse areas and neighborhoods with limited retail competition. We discuss policy options—targeted credits, improved transit links, and place‑based retail incentives—and consider distributional trade‑offs when localities rely on consumption taxes to fund public services.
