About

I'm a health economist and data scientist focused on turning complex administrative and survey data into evidence that decision‑makers can act on. My current research examines how the Affordable Care Act's Medicaid expansion changed coverage, access, and use of care for U.S.-born and foreign‑born adults. Methodologically, I work with modern difference‑in‑differences and event‑study designs that account for staggered policy timing, and I pair those designs with survey‑weighted estimation and design‑based standard errors. I enjoy the unglamorous parts of analysis—clean measurement, careful checks for pre‑trends, robustness to alternative cohorts and specifications, and transparent uncertainty communication. Beyond research, I'm motivated by work that lowers administrative burden and makes programs easier to use, particularly for immigrants and other groups who face documentation and language barriers. I have experience collaborating with clinicians and policy teams, teaching microeconomics and labor economics, and building lightweight tools in R and Python to share results. I value open, reproducible workflows, clear writing, and pragmatic recommendations that acknowledge trade‑offs. I'm based in Ann Arbor, MI, work primarily remotely, and am open to short travel for collaboration and presentations.
